# Like vs As

Both used for comparison, but different grammar.

## Like (Preposition)

Followed by noun/pronoun:
- *She looks **like** her mother.*
- *He runs **like** the wind.*
- *It tastes **like** chicken.*
- *I want a car **like** yours.*

Meaning: similar to, resembling

## As (Conjunction)

Followed by clause (subject + verb):
- ***As** I said, it's important.*
- *Do it **as** I showed you.*
- ***As** you know, we're late.*

## As (Preposition) - Role/Function

Meaning "in the role/capacity of":
- *She works **as** a teacher.* (= she IS a teacher)
- *He used the box **as** a table.* (= functioning as)
- ***As** your friend, I must tell you...*

Compare:
- *She looks **like** a teacher.* (resembles)
- *She works **as** a teacher.* (is one)

## As...As (Equality)

- *She's **as** tall **as** her brother.*
- *It's not **as** good **as** before.*

## Common Expressions

### With "like"
- like crazy, like mad
- feel like, look like, smell like

### With "as"
- as usual, as always
- as you know, as I said
- such as (giving examples)
- as if/as though (hypothetical)

## Modern Usage

"Like" increasingly used before clauses (informal):
- *Like I said...* (informal)
- *As I said...* (formal)

## Common Mistakes

- ❌ *She works like a teacher.*
- ✅ *She works as a teacher.*
